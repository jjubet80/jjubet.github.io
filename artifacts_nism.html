Initial Post

Research was conducted by a team of computing students to demonstrate the viability of breaching a production-deployed medical training mannequin. The research was carried out with an active medical training device in the College of Nursing at the University of South Alabama. Why this was important is because of the increasing integration of technology into medical field creating new avenues for malicious attacks with potentially serious cybercrime consequences.

The research demonstrated that a brute force attack and a denial-of-service (DoS) attack can be successfully carried out on a Medical Mannequin via the network communication between the medical mannequin and the controlling computer. The tool used was a medical mannequin called Istan mannequin, which uses the TCP network protocol and 802.11 wireless transmissions between the controlling laptops and the iStan’s internal access point

The Denial-of-service (DoS) attack works by sending many TCP requests by an attacker to a destination device and in this case, a script called mdk3 was used to send disconnect signals to the access point on iStan, which evidenced connection error message during the DoS attack. In mitigating this type of attack, one approach is to reduce the time-out period for request to prune the connection in the TCP queue (Schuba et al., 1997). In this case, since the communication is between a dedicated computer and the Istan, a very low time-out can be set which is only necessary for a communication between the Istan and the dedicated computer within its network. A study of the best connection time-out will be done to get the desired time without risk of dropping legitimate packets in slow connections (Qualys Security Blog, 2011). This will also come with more bandwidth and connection speed requirement between the computer and the Istan.

Monitoring and filtering activities can be set up with the aim of identifying unusual traffic and filtering them. One method is the History-based filtering. An understanding of a normal traffic as well the devices that connect to the Medical Mannequin is maintained. Unusual Ip addresses or traffic are then filtered and discarded (Mahjabin et al., 2017)

A brute force attack works by guessing login details to access the system. The report shows that the password length adopted is 8 characters and apparently, the access point ID is the same as the password. It took just 2 hrs to get these login Information. A password policy can be adopted to ensure only strong passwords can be used to access the system. For example, Minimum password length should be at least 12 characters, include both upper and lower case letters, include numeric characters and include some special characters like &,#,@,$,% (Dave, 2013).

Another approach is to adopt the two factor or multi factor authentication such that aside the PIN and ID of the Access point, a second level authentication is implemented using other means like one time password or behavioural recognition (Ometov et al., 2018). The attacker, who might be able the guess password will need to also provide a second level authentication hence will be discouraged from using brute force.

Although Medical mannequin may be a simulated medical environment, there is the risk of cybercriminals tampering with medical training information to incorrectly assess medical situations and provide inaccurate feedback hence, understanding the security implication of an unprotected medical equipment that is integrated into the internet, will not only prevent breaches, but also save lives.

This has proven to be more effective than other methods (Dickson, 2020).
